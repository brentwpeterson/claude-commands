# Spanish Content - Native Mexican Spanish Writing Skill

Write, review, and correct Spanish content for all brands and sites. All output is written as a native Mexican Spanish speaker would write it, never as a translation from English.

## When to Use

- Writing new Spanish content for any site (Content Cucumber, Content Basis, RequestDesk, Talk Commerce)
- Reviewing existing Spanish translations for naturalness
- Converting English content to natural Mexican Spanish
- Creating Spanish versions of JSON landing pages
- Writing Spanish blog posts, emails, social media, or any marketing content

## Usage

```
/spanish-content write "<topic or brief>"          Write new Spanish content
/spanish-content review <file-path>                Review and fix existing Spanish content
/spanish-content convert <file-path>               Convert English file to natural Spanish
/spanish-content scan <file-or-dir>                Scan for issues, report only (no auto-fix)
/spanish-content --help                            Show help
```

## Core Principle

**We rewrite. We do not translate.**

Taking English content and swapping words into Spanish produces copy that reads like a translation. It keeps English sentence structure, English rhythm, and English idioms with Spanish words on top. That is not what we do.

When English source material exists, we read it for intent and message, then close it and write the Spanish version from scratch. The result should read like it was written by a native Mexican Spanish speaker for a Mexican audience. If someone handed the Spanish version to a reader with no context, it should never feel like it came from English.

The difference:

| Translation (wrong) | Rewrite (correct) |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| "Construimos motores de contenido para marcas en crecimiento" | "Ayudamos a marcas en crecimiento a generar contenido que realmente funciona" |
| "Escritores humanos que aprenden tu voz" | "Escritores de carne y hueso que entienden tu marca" |
| "Cero contenido de IA" | "Sin relleno generado por inteligencia artificial" |
| "Mas informacion" | "Más información" |

Translation preserves English sentence structure. A rewrite restructures for how Spanish speakers actually communicate.

## Voice and Tone (Inherited from brand-brent)

These principles apply to ALL Spanish content across all brands. They are the Spanish equivalent of the baseline voice rules defined in `/brand-brent`.

### Write for the Problem, Not the Award

Most B2B brands treat social media like a press release channel. Announcements. Awards. New hires. Nobody cares about your award. They care about their problem. Write for the problem. Every single time.

| Wrong (press release voice) | Right (problem-first voice) |
|-----------------------------|----------------------------|
| "Content Cucumber gana premio al mejor contenido B2B" | "Tu contenido no genera leads porque suena igual que el de todos los demás" |
| "Nos aliamos con HubSpot como Solutions Partner" | "¿Tu CRM tiene datos que nadie usa? Hay una forma de convertirlos en contenido que vende" |
| "Lanzamos nuestra nueva plataforma RequestDesk" | "Publicar contenido en 4 canales distintos no debería tomar todo el día" |

### Sell Outcomes, Not Tools

Customers don't care about HubSpot, your 40+ writers, or your tech stack. They care about what those things DO for them. Lead with the buyer's problem, show the outcome.

| Wrong (selling the tool/us) | Right (selling the outcome) |
|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| "Contamos con más de 40 escritores humanos" | "Contenido que suena a tu marca, no a una máquina" |
| "Usamos HubSpot Marketing Hub Pro" | "Ve qué contenido genera ventas, no solo tráfico" |
| "Somos expertos en ecommerce B2B" | "Tus compradores investigan en Reddit, YouTube y ChatGPT antes de llegar a tu sitio. Tu contenido necesita estar ahí" |
| "RequestDesk automatiza la publicación" | "Deja de perder horas publicando lo mismo en 4 plataformas" |

### Client is the Hero

The client is the protagonist. The brand (Content Cucumber, Content Basis, RequestDesk, Commerce King) is the guide. Never position the brand as the star.

| Wrong (brand is hero) | Right (client is hero) |
|-----------------------|------------------------|
| "Somos líderes en contenido para ecommerce" | "Tu marca merece contenido que convierta, no relleno" |
| "Nuestro equipo domina el marketing B2B" | "Tú conoces tu producto. Nosotros te ayudamos a que el mundo lo conozca" |

### Active Voice Preferred

Spanish naturally uses more passive constructions than English. In marketing copy, lean toward active voice. Save passive for variety, not as default.

| Passive (avoid as default) | Active (preferred) |
|----------------------------|-------------------|
| "El contenido es generado por nuestro equipo" | "Nuestro equipo genera tu contenido" |
| "Los resultados fueron comprobados por los datos" | "Los datos comprueban los resultados" |
| "Las campañas son optimizadas semanalmente" | "Optimizamos tus campañas cada semana" |

### Specific Examples Over Generic Claims

Concrete numbers and real scenarios beat vague promises every time.

| Generic (weak) | Specific (strong) |
|----------------|-------------------|
| "Ofrecemos soluciones de migración de clase mundial" | "Migramos 12,000 SKUs sin tiempo de inactividad" |
| "Mejoramos tu posicionamiento en buscadores" | "De página 4 a los primeros 3 resultados en 90 días" |
| "Generamos contenido de alta calidad" | "8 artículos al mes, revisados por editores, listos para publicar en tu blog y redes" |

### Never Use (Spanish Banned Phrases)

These are the Spanish equivalents of the English "Never Use" list from brand-brent. They sound like AI filler, corporate boilerplate, or translated marketing copy.

**AI/Corporate Filler (never use):**
- "En el mundo actual" / "En la era digital"
- "Soluciones innovadoras" / "Soluciones integrales"
- "De vanguardia" / "De clase mundial"
- "Potenciar tu negocio" / "Llevar al siguiente nivel"
- "Revolucionario" / "Transformador" / "Disruptivo"
- "Sinergia" / "Apalancamiento" (calque of "leverage")
- "Cambio de paradigma"
- "En el vertiginoso mundo de"
- "El panorama actual"
- "Hemos sido testigos de"

**Vague Hook Openers (never use):**
- "Algo increíble pasó"
- "Lo que nadie te dice sobre..."
- "No vas a creer lo que..."
- "El secreto que nadie comparte"
- "X acaba de hacer algo grande"
- "La mayoría no se dio cuenta de esto"

Open with the fact. Say what happened, when, and where. Let the reader decide if it's interesting.

**AI Rhetorical Patterns (never use):**
- "La verdadera pregunta es..." (just ask the question)
- "El patrón es claro." (show the pattern, don't announce it)
- "Lee eso otra vez." (if emphasis needed, use "Lo repito:")
- "Esto lo cambia todo." (let the reader judge)
- "Aquí es donde se pone interesante" (just say the interesting thing)

**Filler Words (never use as padding):**
- "Realmente" (when used as filler, not emphasis)
- "Básicamente"
- "Literalmente" (when not literal)
- "Actualmente" (when it adds nothing)
- "Definitivamente" (when hedging, not affirming)

**Sycophantic/Agreement Filler (never use):**
- "Sin duda alguna"
- "Es importante destacar que"
- "Cabe mencionar que"
- "Vale la pena señalar que"

### No Vague Hooks

Same rule as English. Open with the fact. Never with mystery or manufactured curiosity.

| Wrong (vague hook) | Right (fact-first) |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| "Algo está cambiando en el ecommerce B2B" | "El 68% de compradores B2B investigan en YouTube antes de contactar a un proveedor" |
| "Lo que aprendimos nos sorprendió" | "Descubrimos que el contenido en español genera 3x más engagement en LATAM que el contenido traducido" |

## Mexican Spanish Rules (Mandatory)

### 1. Orthography (Non-negotiable)

Every accent, every tilde, every inverted punctuation mark. No exceptions.

| Wrong | Correct | Why |
|-------|---------|-----|
| anos | años | "anos" means anuses. "años" means years. |
| mas | más | Without accent, "mas" means "but" (literary). "Más" means "more". |
| informacion | información | All -ción words take accent. |
| creacion | creación | Same rule. |
| que (in questions) | qué | Interrogative pronouns always accented. |
| como (in questions) | cómo | Same rule. |
| esta (verb) | está | Third person present of "estar" takes accent. |
| tu (possessive before noun) | tu | No accent. "Tú" (with accent) = "you" as subject. |
| si (conditional) | sí (affirmative) | "Si" = if. "Sí" = yes. |

### 1b. Capitalization (Non-negotiable)

**Spanish does NOT capitalize titles like English.** Only the first word and proper nouns are capitalized.

| English (title case) | Spanish (sentence case) | Why |
|----------------------|------------------------|-----|
| "How We Build With AI" | "Cómo construimos con IA" | Only first word capitalized |
| "Frequently Asked Questions" | "Preguntas frecuentes" | "frecuentes" is not a proper noun |
| "All Services" | "Todos los servicios" | No title case in Spanish |
| "Brand Ecosystem" | "Ecosistema de marcas" | Only first word |
| "Competitive Intelligence" | "Inteligencia competitiva" | Only first word |
| "Platform Capabilities" | "Capacidades de plataforma" | Only first word |

**What stays capitalized:**
- First word of any heading or title
- Proper nouns: Adobe Commerce, CommerceKing, HubSpot, LoopStack, BigCommerce
- Acronyms: CMS, IA, QA, CRM, SEO, AEO
- "Ecommerce" when used as a brand-adjacent term (context-dependent)

**What NEVER gets capitalized in Spanish titles:**
- Articles: el, la, los, las, un, una
- Prepositions: de, en, con, por, para, sin, sobre
- Conjunctions: y, o, pero, que
- Common nouns and adjectives (even if English capitalizes them in titles)

**Question marks:** Always use ¿ at the start. "¿Listo para empezar?" not "Listo para empezar?"

**Exclamation marks:** Always use ¡ at the start. "¡Hablamos español!" not "Hablamos español!"

### 2. Formality: Tú vs Usted

**Default: Tú** (informal) for all consumer-facing content. This is standard in Mexican digital marketing.

**Use Usted only when:**
- The content explicitly targets C-suite executives in formal contexts
- Legal or compliance content
- Government-facing content
- The user explicitly requests formal tone

**Never mix** tú and usted in the same page.

### 3. Words That Stay in English

These terms are used in English throughout Latin American business. Do NOT translate them:

**Always English:**
- marketing, email, blog, podcast, SEO, AEO, CRM, SaaS, B2B, B2C, DTC
- ecommerce (not "comercio electrónico" unless in formal/legal contexts)
- landing page, call to action, lead, funnel, pipeline
- branding, storytelling, engagement, feedback
- startup, pitch, sprint, roadmap
- dashboard, analytics, tracking
- HubSpot, Shopify, Magento, BigCommerce, WooCommerce, WordPress
- ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews
- Content Cucumber, Content Basis, RequestDesk, Talk Commerce, Commerce King

**Translate these (common mistake to leave in English):**
- "content" → "contenido"
- "team" → "equipo"
- "partner" → "socio" or "aliado"
- "service" → "servicio"
- "customer" → "cliente"
- "brand" → "marca"
- "writer" → "escritor/escritora" or "redactor/redactora"

### 4. Mexican Spanish Phrasing

**Prefer natural Mexican expressions:**

| Stiff translation | Natural Mexican Spanish |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| "Estamos listos para ayudarte" | "¿Listo para arrancar?" |
| "Contáctanos hoy" | "Platiquemos" or "Escríbenos" |
| "Nuestra solución" | "Lo que hacemos por ti" |
| "Implementamos estrategias" | "Ponemos manos a la obra" |
| "Obtenga resultados" | "Ve los resultados tú mismo" |
| "Sin compromiso" | "Sin letra chiquita" |
| "Agenda una llamada" | "Agenda una llamada" (this one is fine as-is) |

**Avoid overly formal or Spain-Spanish phrasing:**

| Spain Spanish (avoid) | Mexican Spanish (use) |
|-----------------------|----------------------|
| "vosotros" | Never. Use "ustedes". |
| "ordenador" | "computadora" |
| "móvil" | "celular" |
| "vale" | "órale", "sale", "va" (or just omit) |
| "coger" | NEVER use this word in Mexican Spanish. Use "tomar", "agarrar", "obtener". |
| "gilipollas" | Not used in Mexico. |
| "mola" | Not used in Mexico. |

### 5. Sentence Structure

**Spanish sentences are naturally longer than English.** Do not force short, choppy English-style sentences. Spanish flows with subordinate clauses connected by commas.

**But also:** Do not write paragraphs that are walls of text. Break at natural thought boundaries.

**Good rhythm:**
> Llevamos más de 30 años en el mundo del ecommerce, y en todo ese tiempo hemos visto lo mismo una y otra vez: las marcas que invierten en contenido de calidad son las que terminan ganando. No hay atajos.

**Too choppy (English rhythm forced into Spanish):**
> Tenemos 30 años en ecommerce. Hemos visto lo mismo. Las marcas que invierten ganan. No hay atajos.

**Too dense:**
> Llevamos más de 30 años en el mundo del ecommerce y en todo ese tiempo hemos visto lo mismo una y otra vez que las marcas que invierten en contenido de calidad son las que terminan ganando porque no hay atajos y el contenido consistente es lo que construye autoridad a largo plazo.

### 6. Gender-Inclusive Language

Use masculine as default for generic plurals (standard in Mexican business writing). Do not use:
- "escritores/escritoras" (pick one based on context)
- "l@s", "lxs", "les" as gender-neutral markers (not standard in Mexican business)
- Unless the user explicitly requests inclusive language

### 7. Numbers and Currency

- Use comma for thousands: 10,000 (not 10.000 as in Spain)
- Use period for decimals: $99.99
- Currency: Use USD or "dólares" unless targeting a specific country
- Percentages: 40% (no space before %)

## Subcommand: `review`

Review an existing Spanish file for issues.

1. Read the file
2. Check for:
   - Missing accents (á, é, í, ó, ú, ñ)
   - Missing ¿ and ¡
   - Translated-from-English phrasing (restructure to natural Spanish)
   - Spain Spanish vs Mexican Spanish issues
   - Tú/usted consistency
   - Terms that should stay in English but were translated
   - Terms that should be in Spanish but were left in English
3. Show a summary of issues found
4. Fix them all
5. Write the corrected file

Output format:
```
## Spanish Content Review: {filename}

**Issues found:** {count}

| Line | Issue | Before | After |

| ... | Unnatural phrasing | "Construimos motores de contenido" | "Creamos sistemas de contenido que funcionan" |

**Fixed and saved to:** {filepath}
```

## Subcommand: `convert`

Rewrite English content as native Mexican Spanish. This is a rewrite, not a translation.

1. Read the English source file for intent and message
2. Close it. Do not look at it sentence by sentence.
3. Write the Spanish version from scratch as a native speaker would write it for a Mexican audience
4. Apply all voice, tone, and Mexican Spanish rules
5. Save as `{filename}-es.{ext}` in the same directory

**Process:**
- Read the English content to understand what it says and why
- Close the English content mentally
- Write the Spanish version as if you were writing it originally for a Mexican business audience
- The structure, sentence count, paragraph breaks, and section order can all change. Match the message, not the shape.
- Check accents and punctuation
- Save

**The test:** If a native Spanish reader can tell it came from English, the rewrite failed.

## Subcommand: `scan`

Scan Spanish content for issues and produce a diagnostic report. Does NOT modify any files.

**Usage:**
```
/spanish-content scan <file>           Scan a single file
/spanish-content scan <directory>      Scan all .json, .astro, .md files in directory
```

**Process:**
1. Read every file in scope
2. Run all checks below against each Spanish string
3. Output a categorized report sorted by severity
4. Do NOT fix anything. Report only.

**Check categories (in order of severity):**

### Critical (breaks meaning)
- **Missing accents:** `anos` vs `años`, `mas` vs `más`, `informacion` vs `información`, etc.
- **Missing ñ:** Any word that needs ñ but has plain n
- **Missing ¿ or ¡:** Questions without opening ¿, exclamations without opening ¡
- **Dangerous words:** `coger` (must be `tomar`/`agarrar`/`obtener`), `vosotros`

### High (sounds like a translation, not native Spanish)
- **English-style title capitalization:** In Spanish, only the first word and proper nouns are capitalized in titles/headings. Flag any multi-word heading where non-proper-noun words are capitalized (e.g., "Desarrollo De Comercio Digital" should be "Desarrollo de comercio digital")
- **Translated-from-English phrasing:** Sentences that follow English word order or use English idioms forced into Spanish (e.g., "construimos motores de contenido" instead of natural restructuring)
- **Banned phrases:** Any phrase from the "Never Use" lists (AI filler, vague hooks, sycophantic filler, etc.)
- **Terms left in English that should be Spanish:** "content" instead of "contenido", "team" instead of "equipo", etc.
- **Terms translated that should stay English:** SEO, marketing, ecommerce, blog, CRM, etc.

### Medium (style/polish)
- **Tú/usted inconsistency:** Mixed formality within the same page or file
- **Passive voice overuse:** More than 2 passive constructions in a row
- **Spain Spanish instead of Mexican:** `ordenador`, `móvil`, `vale`, `mola`
- **Em dashes:** Any use of the em dash character
- **Filler words:** `realmente`, `básicamente`, `literalmente`, `actualmente`, `definitivamente` used as padding
- **Generic claims without specifics:** "soluciones de clase mundial", "líderes en la industria"

### Low (minor polish)
- **Number/currency formatting:** 10.000 instead of 10,000 (Mexican format uses comma for thousands)
- **Percentage spacing:** Space before % (should be no space: 40%)
- **Emoji presence** (unless explicitly requested)

**Output format:**
```
## Spanish Scan Report: {filename or directory}

**Files scanned:** {count}
**Total issues:** {count} ({critical} critical, {high} high, {medium} medium, {low} low)

### Critical ({count})
| File | Line/Key | Issue | Found | Expected |
|------|----------|-------|-------|----------|
| es.json | hero.headline1 | English-style caps | "Desarrollo De" | "Desarrollo de" |

### High ({count})
| File | Line/Key | Issue | Found | Suggestion |
|------|----------|-------|-------|------------|
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

### Medium ({count})
| File | Line/Key | Issue | Found | Note |
|------|----------|-------|-------|------|
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

### Low ({count})
| File | Line/Key | Issue | Found | Note |
|------|----------|-------|-------|------|
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

### Summary
- Accent issues: {count}
- Capitalization issues: {count}
- Naturalness issues: {count}
- Banned phrase hits: {count}
- Term language issues: {count}
```

**Capitalization rule detail (Spanish vs English):**

In English, titles capitalize most words: "How We Build With AI"
In Spanish, only the first word and proper nouns capitalize: "Cómo construimos con IA"

**Common patterns to flag:**
- "Desarrollo De Comercio Digital" should be "Desarrollo de comercio digital"
- "Preguntas Frecuentes" should be "Preguntas frecuentes"
- "Todos Los Servicios" should be "Todos los servicios"
- "Plataformas Ecommerce" should be "Plataformas ecommerce"
- Proper nouns stay capitalized: "Adobe Commerce", "CommerceKing", "HubSpot"

**After scanning:** Present the report and ask the user which issues to fix. Never auto-fix from a scan.

## Quality Checklist

Before saving any Spanish content, verify:

- [ ] All accents present (á, é, í, ó, ú)
- [ ] All ñ where needed (especially "años", "español", "diseño", "señal")
- [ ] ¿ before every question
- [ ] ¡ before every exclamation
- [ ] Consistent tú/usted throughout
- [ ] No "coger" (use tomar/obtener/agarrar)
- [ ] No vosotros
- [ ] Technical terms in English where appropriate (SEO, marketing, ecommerce, blog)
- [ ] Natural sentence rhythm (not English structure forced into Spanish)
- [ ] Brand names preserved in English
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] No emojis
